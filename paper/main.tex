\begin{abstract}
Online platforms have enabled clinical mental health research at an unprecedented scale, yet large language models (LLMs) now threaten the integrity of remotely collected data. Recent work has demonstrated that LLM-based synthetic respondents can complete general surveys and behavioral tasks with human-like plausibility, but the vulnerability of validated clinical psychiatric instruments remains unexamined. Here, we show that a commercially available LLM, given only brief diagnostic persona descriptions, produces clinically differentiated responses across seven standardized psychiatric screening instruments spanning domains such as mood, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive, trauma, psychotic, neurodegenerative, and eating disorders. Synthetic respondents assigned to specific diagnoses generated elevated scores on the corresponding clinical measures, with scores scaling monotonically with assigned severity. Crucially, this mimicry required no model fine-tuning. This low barrier to fabrication suggests that coherent symptom endorsement can no longer serve as a proxy for authentic human nosology, urgently necessitating the development of standardized verification methods tailored to clinical online data.
\end{abstract}

Online platforms and crowdsourcing services have transformed how clinical researchers study mental health\cite{gillan2021}. Surveys administered through services such as Amazon Mechanical Turk and Prolific enable rapid, low-cost access to large and diverse samples, including populations that are difficult to reach through traditional recruitment due to stigma, geographic barriers, or low base rates\cite{chandler2016}. This infrastructure has become particularly important for research on psychiatric conditions, where validated screening instruments commonly serve as gatekeepers for study eligibility and primary outcome measures. However, the assumption underlying such research---that coherent, contextually appropriate survey responses require a human respondent---is no longer tenable.

Westwood recently demonstrated that an autonomous LLM-based synthetic respondents can complete online surveys with high fidelity\cite{westwood2025}. These LLM-based agents passed attention checks, produced consistent psychometric profiles, and evaded standard detection methods. Critically, each completed survey cost approximately \$0.005, making large-scale fabrication economically trivial. Van der Stigchel and colleagues extended this concern beyond self-report, showing that AI-generated responding in online behavioral experiments can even mimic human reaction time distributions\cite{vanderstigchel2026}. Moreover, it does not take a sophisticated user to employ modern AI tools: semi-autonomous AI agents are now built into many browsers and can take a survey semi-autonomously with as little as the click of a button\cite{ning_survey_2025}. While the current scale of AI contamination on online research platforms remains uncertain, these initial probes demonstrate that the threat to online research is broad and deepening. Yet none of these studies examined whether LLMs can produce plausible responses on the clinical instruments that mental health researchers regularly rely on to screen, characterize, and study psychiatric populations.

Clinical psychiatric instruments may be especially vulnerable to LLM mimicry for several reasons. First, mental health content, including descriptions of symptom presentations, diagnostic criteria, and self-report questionnaire items, is extensively represented in the text corpora used to train these models \cite{Jin2025ApplicationsLLMsMentalHealth}. For example, word embeddings trained exclusively on LLM-generated synthetic self-reports can predict mental health screening scores with high accuracy\cite{moell2026}, suggesting that even synthetic clinical text encodes diagnostic information. Second, many clinical studies recruit participants online precisely because the populations of interest (e.g., individuals with eating disorders, psychosis-spectrum experiences, or post-traumatic stress) are difficult to access through other means\cite{chandler2016}. Third, researchers routinely rely on published cutoff scores to validate study eligibility, treating coherent symptom endorsement as implicit proof of a genuine respondent. An agent need only exceed a known threshold to infiltrate a clinical sample, turning quality-control metrics into an attack vector.

To evaluate this vulnerability, we conducted a simulation study in which a commercially available LLM (Google Gemini 2.0 Flash) was used to generate responses to seven validated clinical instruments. We created 2,028 synthetic personas drawn from a representative, yet non-exhaustive, list of 13 diagnostic profiles spanning a wide range of psychiatric conditions and neurodegenerative disorders: major depressive disorder, bipolar I disorder, alcohol-induced mood disorder, generalized anxiety disorder, post-traumatic stress disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, schizophrenia, anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder, Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease dementia, and frontotemporal dementia. Each persona was assigned a severity level (mild, moderate, or severe) and demographic characteristics (age, gender, education, political affiliation). The model received a straightforward system prompt describing the persona's clinical profile, demographic background, and instructions to respond consistently. No adversarial prompt engineering, or fine-tuning was employed. Each persona then completed the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9\cite{kroenke2001phq9}, Generalized Anxiety Disorder Screener  (GAD-7)\cite{spitzer2006gad7}, Obsessive-Compulsive Inventory--Revised (OCI-R)\cite{foa2002ocir}, PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5)\cite{blevins015}, Mood Disorder Questionnaire (MDQ)\cite{hirschfeld2000mdq}, Prodromal Questionnaire-16 (PQ-16)\cite{ising2012pq16}, and Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire (EDE-Q)\cite{fairburn2008edeq}.

We found that LLMs produced clinically differentiated response patterns across all instruments (Fig.~\ref{fig:1}), such that each diagnostic persona exhibited elevated scores on the measures most conceptually aligned with its assigned clinical profile. Importantly, assigned severity modulated scores in the expected direction: personas assigned greater diagnostic severity consistently produced higher scores than moderate or mild ones. This pattern of diagnostic specificity and severity sensitivity was achieved entirely through the model's pretrained knowledge---the prompt contained no information about instrument content, scoring rubrics, or clinical cutoffs.
 
The triviality of generating plausible counterfeits illustrates the severity of this threat to mental health research. Because a standard commercial LLM can semi-autonomously produce clinically differentiated profiles with zero specialized configuration, the economic incentives for survey fraud are now sharply asymmetric: fabricating a large, threshold-crossing clinical cohort costs almost nothing relative to the high costs of genuine clinical data collection. With common quality-control tools already shown to be insufficient\cite{westwood2025}, the clinical setting compounds this problem: synthetic respondents that produce scores above established screening cutoffs would be indistinguishable from genuine participants on the very metrics researchers use to verify sample composition. Our findings do not demonstrate that such contamination is currently occurring at scale, nor do we claim that our simulation exercise captures the full complexity of authentic clinical presentations. Rather, we highlight that the technical barrier to generating plausible clinical data is remarkably low, and that the field's current defenses may not be designed to address this class of threat. Researchers should consider implementing identity verification beyond attention checks, triangulating self-report with behavioral or physiological measures, avoiding diagnosis-specific language in recruitment materials, and treating unverified online panel data with caution \cite{quagan_navigating_2021}. Developing standardized detection methods calibrated to clinical response patterns is therefore an urgent priority.

\section*{Online Methods}

\subsection*{Synthetic Persona Generation}

We generated 2,028 unique synthetic personas by taking the set of all pairs of 13 clinical diagnostic profiles, 3 severity levels (mild, moderate, severe), 3 age groups (25, 50, 75), 2 genders (male, female), 3 education levels (high school, bachelor's degree, master's degree), and 3 political affiliations (Democrat, Republican, Independent). Each clinical profile specified a DSM-informed diagnosis and a set of characteristic symptoms. The 13 diagnoses spanned mood disorders (major depressive disorder, bipolar I disorder, alcohol-induced mood disorder), anxiety disorders (generalized anxiety disorder, post-traumatic stress disorder), obsessive-compulsive disorder, psychotic disorders (schizophrenia, prodromal syndrome), eating disorders (anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder), and neurodegenerative conditions (Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease dementia, frontotemporal dementia). Each persona was assigned a unique identifier encoding its diagnostic code, severity, gender, age, education, and political affiliation.

\subsection*{Clinical Assessment Batteries}

Each persona completed seven clinical instruments:
(1) the Patient Health Questionnaire--9 (PHQ-9; 9 items, 0--3 scale)\cite{kroenke2001phq9};
(2) the Generalized Anxiety Disorder--7 (GAD-7; 7 items, 0--3 scale)\cite{spitzer2006gad7};
(3) the Obsessive-Compulsive Inventory--Revised (OCI-R; 18 items, 0--4 scale)\cite{foa2002ocir};
(4) the PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5; 20 items, 0--4 scale)\cite{blevins015};
(5) the Mood Disorder Questionnaire (MDQ; 13 yes/no items)\cite{hirschfeld2000mdq};
(6) the Prodromal Questionnaire--16 (PQ-16; 16 true/false items)\cite{ising2012pq16};
and (7) the Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire (EDE-Q 6.0; 28 items, mixed frequency and attitudinal scales)\cite{fairburn2008edeq}.
All items and response options were implemented as specified in the published instruments.

\subsection*{Simulation Procedure}

Simulations were conducted using the Google Gemini 2.0 Flash API. For each persona, we initialized a full profile (diagnosis, symptoms, severity, demographics) and an initially empty response history. For each battery, similar to \cite{westwood2025}, the model received a system prompt containing: (1) the persona profile; (2) all prior responses from previous batteries, ensuring cross-instrument consistency; (3) the current battery's questions with their response options; and (4) instructions to respond in character, maintain consistency with prior answers, limit text responses to 20 words, and calibrate language to the persona's education level. The model was instructed to return one response per question. All batteries were administered to each persona.

\subsection*{Scoring}

Raw text responses were mapped to scores using standard scoring rubrics for each instrument. For the PHQ-9 and GAD-7, responses were scored 0 (Not at all) through 3 (Nearly every day) and summed. For the OCI-R, responses were scored 0 (Not at all) through 4 (Extremely) and summed. For the PCL-5, responses were scored 0 (Not at all) through 4 (Extremely) and summed. For the MDQ, Yes/No responses were coded as 1/0 and summed across the 13 screening items. For the PQ-16, True/False responses were coded as 1/0 and summed. For the EDE-Q, frequency items were scored on a 0-6 scale corresponding to the number of days in the past 28, attitudinal items were mapped to the standard 7-point scale. Global EDE-Q scores were computed as the mean of the four subscale averages (Restraint, Eating Concern, Shape Concern, and Weight Concern), following standard scoring procedures\cite{fairburn2008edeq}.

\subsection*{Data and Code Availability}

All simulation code, scoring scripts, and raw data are available at \url{https://github.com/kiante-fernandez/LLM-clinical}.
